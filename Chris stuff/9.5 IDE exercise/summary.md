## 1. Introduction

This manual focuses on adults hospitalised with advanced HIV disease (AHD), a high-risk group that still presents frequently despite universal ART recommendations. AHD is defined mainly by a CD4 count below 200 cells/mm3 or WHO stage 3 or 4 disease.

The guideline exists because hospitalised patients with AHD often present with multiple overlapping opportunistic infections and higher mortality risk than ambulatory patients. Its purpose is to give practical diagnostic and management approaches for common inpatient AHD problems, especially in the South African setting.

## 2. Renal Impairment

Renal dysfunction in AHD should not be judged by creatinine alone. The guideline recommends estimating GFR, usually with Cockcroft-Gault or CKD-EPI, and remembering that dolutegravir can raise creatinine without causing true renal decline.

The first clinical distinction is acute kidney injury versus chronic kidney disease. AKI needs urgent correction of reversible causes such as dehydration, sepsis, obstruction, or nephrotoxic drugs. CKD management focuses more on long-term risk reduction, blood pressure control, HIV suppression, avoidance of nephrotoxins, and dose adjustment of renally cleared drugs.

Practical inpatient AKI management includes fluids when appropriate, stopping nephrotoxins, adjusting medication doses, and investigating common HIV-related causes such as pre-renal injury, ATN, TIN, obstruction, and HIVAN. ART dosing may need adjustment in renal failure, especially with tenofovir and lamivudine.

## 3. Diarrhoea

Diarrhoea is common and dangerous in AHD because it causes dehydration, electrolyte losses, admission, and death. The chapter separates acute diarrhoea from chronic diarrhoea and emphasises careful history, hydration assessment, HIV status, stool features, and signs suggesting small bowel versus large bowel disease.

Causes include infectious pathogens such as Cryptosporidium, Cystoisospora, Microsporidia, Giardia, amoebiasis, bacterial enteritis, C. difficile, TB, MAC, and CMV, as well as non-infectious causes such as drugs, malabsorption, and malignancy. Initial workup is driven by severity and chronicity, with bloods, stool studies, selected imaging, and endoscopy when symptoms persist despite negative first-line tests.

Management is supportive first: rehydration, electrolyte correction, and targeted treatment when a cause is found. The guideline repeatedly stresses that immune reconstitution is central, especially for cryptosporidiosis, and that ART should not be delayed unnecessarily once serious contraindicating OIs are excluded.

## 4. Cerebral Space Occupying Lesions

In AHD, brain space-occupying lesions commonly present with seizures or focal deficits. The main differentials are TB, toxoplasmosis, pyogenic abscess, neurocysticercosis, lymphoma, cryptococcoma, nocardia, and metastatic disease.

If CT is available, management starts with identifying emergencies such as hydrocephalus, herniation, mass effect, or pyogenic abscess requiring neurosurgical input. If CT is not pathognomonic, the guideline recommends parallel investigation for toxoplasmosis, TB, cryptococcosis, and syphilis, plus lumbar puncture only when safe.

Empiric treatment is driven by the most likely diagnosis and adjusted as results return. Toxoplasmosis is often treated first while awaiting tests unless features strongly support TB. Follow-up imaging after about 2 weeks helps determine whether lesions are shrinking, unchanged, or worsening, and ART timing depends on the confirmed diagnosis.

## 5. Acute and Sub-Acute/Chronic Meningitis

The guideline separates acute meningitis from sub-acute or chronic meningitis mainly by symptom duration, while noting overlap. Acute meningitis is a medical emergency: give ceftriaxone immediately, add ampicillin when Listeria is suspected, and do not delay antibiotics for CT.

Lumbar puncture is essential unless contraindicated by reduced consciousness, papilloedema, focal deficits, recent seizures, severe instability, coagulopathy, or local sepsis. Core tests include opening pressure, CSF cell count, glucose, protein, gram stain, culture, CrAg, serum glucose, CRP, and stored CSF for later testing.

For acute disease, the manual recommends reassessing after 48 to 72 hours using clinical response and repeat CSF if needed. For chronic meningitis, the main treatable causes in this setting are TB meningitis, cryptococcal meningitis, and neurosyphilis, while malignancy and inflammatory disorders remain important alternatives.

## 6. Respiratory Presentation

Most hospitalised AHD patients with respiratory disease have TB, bacterial pneumonia, PJP, or combinations of these. The central task is early empiric treatment while using imaging, blood counts, rapid TB diagnostics, and oxygenation status to narrow the differential.

TB is the commonest diagnosis and often disseminated. Helpful clues include anaemia, characteristic ultrasound or CXR findings, positive urine LAM, sputum or induced sputum Xpert Ultra, and accessible lymph node sampling. Highly suggestive cases should start TB treatment early.

Bacterial pneumonia usually resembles severe community-acquired pneumonia and should be treated with broad-spectrum beta-lactams, adding a macrolide in more severe disease. PJP is usually subacute, hypoxic, and associated with bilateral interstitial infiltrates; treatment is high-dose co-trimoxazole plus prednisone in hypoxic patients. The chapter also flags fungal disease, viral infections, nocardia, Kaposi sarcoma, lymphoma, and LIP as alternative causes.

## 7. Altered Mental State

The first diagnostic step is distinguishing delirium, psychosis, and dementia, while remembering that delirium can coexist with either. In hospitalised AHD patients, the default assumption should be delirium until proven otherwise.

Assessment includes collateral history, medication and substance review, full examination, mental state examination, chest X-ray, and blood tests. If suspicion remains for CNS infection, the guideline recommends lumbar puncture unless contraindicated, with CT brain and targeted CSF testing as needed.

Treatable causes include infections such as HSV, VZV, CMV, TB, cryptococcosis, HIV-related CNS disease, sepsis, metabolic derangements, drugs including efavirenz, alcohol, and withdrawal states. If psychosis predominates after delirium and infection are addressed, psychiatric treatment may be needed, while persistent cognitive decline raises concern for HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder.

## 8. Deterioration Despite Treatment for an Opportunistic Infection

When a patient worsens after starting treatment for an OI, the guideline recommends a structured review rather than assuming treatment failure. The first question is whether the drug is actually reaching the organism: check prescribing, dispensing, adherence, absorption, drug interactions, dose adequacy, and whether surgery is needed for poorly penetrated sites such as abscesses.

Next, reconsider microbial resistance and whether the original diagnosis was correct, especially if treatment was empiric. The chapter also stresses looking for a second diagnosis, since patients with AHD often have multiple simultaneous infections or concurrent malignancy.

Other explanations include adverse drug reactions, unrealistic expectations about response time, and immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome. IRIS is a diagnosis of exclusion after these other possibilities are checked, and steroids have limited evidence outside selected forms such as non-neurological paradoxical TB-IRIS.

## 9. Use of Steroids in AHD

The overall message is to avoid routine steroid use in AHD because corticosteroids suppress immunity and can worsen outcomes. Empiric steroids for suspected adrenal insufficiency are discouraged unless a random cortisol is checked first and the clinical picture is strongly suggestive.

Evidence supports steroids only in selected situations. The clearest indications in this guideline are hypoxic PJP, confirmed TTP, treatment of non-neurological paradoxical TB-IRIS, prevention of paradoxical TB-IRIS in selected high-risk patients starting ART soon after TB treatment, and some ICU-managed septic shock or oxygen-requiring COVID-19 cases.

The guideline advises against routine steroid use in cryptococcal meningitis, TB pericarditis, and routine TB meningitis management, and recommends particular caution when Kaposi sarcoma is present.
