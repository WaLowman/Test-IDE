## Key message

People living with HIV whose immune systems have become very weak can get seriously ill, but hospitals can help by finding and treating infections early, starting HIV medicine, and watching carefully for problems. Getting care quickly saves lives.

## Main findings

- **Kidney problems are common.** Some HIV medicines and other drugs can harm the kidneys. Doctors need to check how well the kidneys are working and may need to change or adjust medicines to keep the kidneys safe.

- **Diarrhoea is very frequent and can be dangerous.** It affects most people with AHD and can cause the body to lose too much water and important minerals. Finding the cause — whether an infection or a medicine side effect — helps doctors choose the right treatment. Replacing lost fluids is the most important first step.

- **Brain infections need urgent attention.** A fungal infection called cryptococcal meningitis (an infection of the covering of the brain) is one of the most dangerous. A simple blood test can detect it early, and quick treatment greatly improves the chance of survival.

- **Tuberculosis (TB) is the biggest killer.** TB can affect the lungs, the brain, or other parts of the body. It is sometimes hard to find, so doctors may start TB treatment early while waiting for test results.

- **Some patients feel worse before they feel better.** After starting HIV medicine, the recovering immune system can cause swelling and fever — this is called IRIS. It does not mean the treatment has failed. Doctors know how to manage this and may use certain medicines to reduce the swelling.

## What this means for you

The single most important thing you can do is to start HIV treatment as soon as possible and keep taking it every day. Do not wait until you feel very sick. If you have stopped taking your medicine, it is never too late to start again — visit your nearest clinic. If you or someone you know is very unwell with symptoms such as ongoing diarrhoea, severe headaches, confusion, difficulty breathing, or high fevers, go to the hospital without delay.

## Where to get help

- Visit your **nearest clinic or hospital** if you feel unwell or have not yet started HIV treatment.
- Call the **National AIDS Helpline: 0800 012 322** — free, confidential, and available 24 hours a day.
- Speak to a **community health worker** in your area for support, information, and help getting to a clinic.
